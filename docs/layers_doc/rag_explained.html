Section 01

What is RAG? — The Core Idea

Imagine you're on a quiz show, but you're allowed to bring a mini-encyclopedia. Before each question, you quickly flip to the relevant pages, skim the key paragraphs, and then use your brain to pick the answer. You're not memorizing the whole book — just looking up what you need, when you need it.

RAG does exactly this for an AI: Retrieval (flip to the right pages) + Augmented (add those pages to the question) + Generation (use the AI's brain to answer).

Without RAG: The LLM answers purely from its training data — which might be outdated, incomplete, or simply wrong about obscure facts.

With RAG: The LLM gets relevant Wikipedia passages injected into its prompt. It can point to specific evidence when choosing an answer.

Before vs After — What the LLM Sees

Without RAG — the LLM sees only the question
"""
Question: In what year did the Kingdom of Italy annex Rome?
A) 1861  B) 1870  C) 1882  D) 1895

Answer:
"""
# The LLM must rely entirely on memorized training data.
# If it never saw this fact during training → guesses wrong.
With RAG — the LLM sees question + retrieved evidence
"""
Reference material:

[1] Italian unification (chunk 4, history)
The capture of Rome in 1870 completed Italian unification. The Kingdom
of Italy moved its capital from Florence to Rome following the breach
of Porta Pia on September 20, 1870.

[2] Risorgimento (chunk 2, history)
The Risorgimento culminated with the annexation of the Papal States...

---

Question: In what year did the Kingdom of Italy annex Rome?
A) 1861  B) 1870  C) 1882  D) 1895

Answer:
"""
# The LLM sees "1870" twice in the evidence → confidently picks B.
# Even if it never memorized this fact, the evidence is right there.

The key insight: RAG turns a "closed-book exam" (relying on memory) into an "open-book exam" (relying on evidence). The AI doesn't need to have memorized every fact — it just needs to find the right page.

Section 03

Building the Knowledge Index

Before you can look things up, you need to build the reference book. Imagine collecting Wikipedia articles, cutting them into index-card-sized notes, and filing them in two different filing cabinets: one organized by meaning (FAISS), one organized by keywords (BM25). This one-time preparation happens before any game starts.

1
Harvest article titles from Wikipedia categories (one query per seed category via the MediaWiki API), then fetch each article
Category:Roman_emperors, Category:Chemical_elements, … → ~500–2000 titles per topic → corpus.jsonl
2
Chunk each article into ~300-word passages with 50-word overlap
"World War II" (5000 words) → 18 chunks of ~300 words each
3
Embed each chunk into a 384-dimensional vector (numeric fingerprint)
"Rome was annexed in 1870..." → [0.023, -0.157, 0.890, ... ] (384 numbers)
4
Store in FAISS index (all vectors together for fast similarity search)
knowledge.faiss — one file, thousands of vectors
5
Build BM25 index (keyword lookup table from the same chunks)
knowledge.bm25.json — word → which chunks contain it

Where do the article titles come from? Rather than hand-picking ~95 articles per topic (the old approach), the system now asks Wikipedia: "give me every article in Category:Roman_emperors, Category:Chemical_elements, …" via the MediaWiki categorymembers API. ~20 well-chosen seed categories per topic yield ~500–2000 candidate titles. Each title is then fetched, cleaned, and chunked exactly like before. Result: 5–20× more articles in the index, so the right answer is far more likely to be retrievable — particularly for entity-specific trivia (who discovered X, which film won Y).

For engineers ablating the change: pass --legacy-seeds to scripts/build_rag_index.py (or set INDEX_LEGACY_SEEDS=True in the notebook) to fall back to the original hand-curated TOPIC_SEEDS list. The FAISS .manifest.json records which path was used as corpus_source: "category_graph" | "hand_curated", so a re-loader can tell which index they're working with.

The Manifest System prevents silent corruption. When you build the index, a .manifest.json is saved recording the exact model, prefixes, and chunker version used. At retrieval time, the system checks: "Was this index built with the same embedder I'm using now?" If not → hard error. This catches a subtle but devastating bug: querying with a different model produces garbage scores without any obvious error message.

Section 04

Text Chunking — The Art of Splitting

Imagine photocopying a textbook page to make flashcards. If you cut randomly in the middle of sentences, each card is useless ("Germany surrendered on May 7," | "1945. The war in Europe was..."). But if you cut at natural sentence boundaries, each card has a complete, useful fact. That's what smart chunking does.

Why Not Embed Whole Articles?

Two reasons: (1) Embedding models have token limits (~512 tokens max). (2) A single vector for a 5000-word article loses detail — it averages out all the information into one blurry representation. Short, focused chunks produce sharp, specific vectors.

Bad vs Good Chunking

❌ Bad: Fixed-size character split (ignores sentences)
Chunk 1: "...Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 7,"
Chunk 2: "1945. The war in Europe was over. Japan continued"
→ The date "May 7, 1945" is torn apart. Neither chunk has the complete fact.
✅ Good: Sentence-aware split (your chunker)
Chunk 1: "...Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 7, 1945. The war in Europe was over."
Chunk 2: "Japan continued fighting until August 15, 1945, when Emperor Hirohito announced..."
→ Complete sentences. Complete facts. Each chunk stands alone.

The Overlap Trick

The last 50 words of Chunk N become the first 50 words of Chunk N+1. This means a fact that falls near a boundary appears in both chunks — nothing is lost at the seams.

Chunk 1: [word₁ … word₂₅₀] [word₂₅₁ … word₃₀₀]
↑ overlap zone (50 words) appears in both ↑
Chunk 2:                         [word₂₅₁ … word₃₀₀] [word₃₀₁ … word₅₅₀]

The 4-Step Chunking Pipeline

polimibot/rag/chunker.py — simplified pipeline
def chunk_text(text, source, category=None, chunk_size=300, overlap=50):
    """Split raw text into overlapping, sentence-aware Chunk objects."""

    # Step 1: Split on == Section Headers == (Wikipedia style)
    sections = _split_sections(text)

    # Step 2: Split each section into sentences
    # (regex: split on . ! ? followed by whitespace)
    sentences = _split_sentences(section_text)

    # Step 3: Greedily pack sentences into chunks of ~300 words
    # When next sentence would overflow → flush current buffer as a chunk
    # Carry over last 50 words as overlap for the next chunk
    chunks = _pack_sentences(sentences, chunk_size=300, overlap=50)

    # Step 4: Merge tiny tail chunks (< 60 words) into predecessor
    # A 5-word chunk produces a noisy embedding → merge it away
    chunks = _merge_short_tail(chunks)

    return [Chunk(text=c, source=source, chunk_id=i, category=category)
            for i, c in enumerate(chunks)]
  • Splits on == Header == lines. The header text is prepended to the first chunk of its section so the topic travels with its content.A chunk saying "He was born in 1879" is useless without knowing WHO. The section header provides that context.
  • Regex sentence splitter: splits on . ! ? followed by whitespace. Not perfect ("Mr. Smith") but good enough — a stray split just creates a short chunk that gets merged in step 4.Sentences are the atomic unit of meaning. We never split mid-sentence.
  • Greedy packing: keep adding sentences until adding the next one would exceed 300 words. Then flush. Carry 50 words of overlap.Maximizes chunk size (better embeddings) while respecting the word budget.
  • If the final chunk is tiny (< 60 words), merge it into the previous chunk. A 5-word "See also: External links" would waste a top-K slot.Quality control: every chunk must carry enough information to be useful.

Section 05

Embeddings — GPS Coordinates for Meaning

Think of embeddings as GPS coordinates, but for meaning instead of location. Just as two nearby GPS points mean you're physically close, two nearby embedding vectors mean the texts are semantically similar. "Who invented the telephone?" and "Who created the phone?" are at almost the same GPS coordinates. "What's the weather today?" is on a different continent.

Conceptual: similar meanings → similar vectors
# Each text gets converted to a list of 384 numbers (its "GPS coordinates")
"Who invented the telephone?"  →  [0.023, -0.157,  0.890, ...]  # Point A
"Who created the phone?"       →  [0.031, -0.142,  0.882, ...]  # Point B  ← very close!
"What's the weather today?"    →  [0.912,  0.231, -0.451, ...]  # Point C  ← far away

similarity(A, B) = 0.96   # almost identical meaning → these match!
similarity(A, C) = 0.12   # completely different topic → no match

The Model: BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5

PropertyValueWhy it matters
Dimensions384Each text → 384 numbers. Compact yet expressive.
Size~130 MBFits in CPU RAM. No GPU needed for embedding.
Max tokens512~300 words. This is why we chunk to 300 words!
TypeAsymmetricQueries and passages use different prefixes (see below)

The Asymmetry Trick

Queries and passages are encoded differently. The BGE model was trained with a special instruction prefix on the query side. This prefix subtly rotates the query vector into the "question-asking" part of the embedding space, making it better match "answer-containing" passage vectors. Without the prefix, scores drop 5-15%.

polimibot/rag/embedder.py — asymmetric encoding
@dataclass(frozen=True)
class EmbedderSpec:
    model_name:     str = "BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5"
    normalize:      bool = True     # L2-normalize → dot product = cosine similarity
    query_prefix:   str = "Represent this sentence for searching relevant passages: "
    passage_prefix: str = ""        # passages get NO prefix

class Embedder:
    def encode_query(self, texts: list[str]) -> np.ndarray:
        """Embed queries — WITH the instruction prefix."""
        prefixed = [self.spec.query_prefix + t for t in texts]
        return self._encode(prefixed)

    def encode_passage(self, texts: list[str]) -> np.ndarray:
        """Embed passages — NO prefix."""
        return self._encode(texts)
  • normalize=True scales every vector to length 1 (unit vector). After this, dot product = cosine similarity. FAISS's IndexFlatIP becomes a cosine search.Cosine similarity measures angle between vectors (direction), ignoring magnitude. Two texts with the same meaning but different lengths still match.
  • Queries get a long English instruction prefix. This was what the model was trained with — it expects it.The prefix signals "I'm a question looking for answers" → the model encodes the query vector in the right part of the space.
  • Passages get NO prefix — they're just the raw text. This asymmetry is how the model separates "question space" from "answer space."If you accidentally use the passage prefix on queries (or vice versa), you silently corrupt all scores. The manifest system catches this.
Section 06

Search — Two Parallel Methods

Imagine two librarians searching for a book. Librarian A (FAISS) understands meaning: "find me books about flying machines" → finds a book titled "History of Aviation." Librarian B (BM25) matches keywords literally: "find 'flying machines'" → finds a book that uses those exact words. Neither is perfect alone — A might miss a relevant book that uses different vocabulary, B might miss one that discusses the concept without using those exact words. Together, they cover each other's blind spots.

FAISS — Semantic Search (Dense)

FAISS stores all chunk embeddings (384-dim vectors) in a flat index. At search time:

  1. Embed the query using encode_query() → get a 384-dim vector
  2. Compute dot product against every stored vector (brute-force)
  3. Return the top-K vectors with highest scores (= most similar meaning)

Why brute-force? With a few thousand chunks, exact search takes ~50ms. Approximate methods (like HNSW) only help at millions of vectors. For our corpus size, brute-force is faster (no index-build overhead) and guarantees no relevant passage is accidentally missed.

BM25 — Keyword Search (Sparse)

BM25 is "Ctrl+F on steroids." It tokenizes both query and passages, applies stemming (so "invented" matches "inventing"), and scores based on:

FactorEffectExample
Term Frequency (TF)More occurrences of query word in passage = higher score"telephone" appears 3× → boosted
Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)Rare words are more discriminative = extra boost"telephone" is rarer than "the" → worth more
Length normalizationLong passages aren't unfairly rewarded for having more wordsShort focused chunk > long rambling one

Why Use Both?

ScenarioFAISS winsBM25 wins
"Who created the phone?" → passage says "Bell invented the telephone"✅ Understands synonyms❌ "phone" ≠ "telephone"
"What is Schrödinger's equation?" → passage contains the exact equation name❌ Might match "quantum physics" broadly✅ Exact keyword match
Rare proper nouns: "Who is Valtteri Bottas?"❌ May not have seen this name enough✅ Exact match on "Bottas"
Section 07

RRF Fusion — Merging Two Rankings

Imagine two judges scoring ice skating routines. Judge A (FAISS) scores on artistic impression (0-6 scale). Judge B (BM25) scores on technical merit (0-30 scale). You can't just add their scores — Judge B's numbers are 5× bigger! Instead, RRF ignores the scores entirely and uses only the rank positions: "Who did each judge place 1st, 2nd, 3rd?" A skater ranked highly by BOTH judges is the consensus winner.

The RRF Formula (Cormack et al., 2009)
# RRF score for document d across N ranked lists:
#
#   RRF(d) = Σᵢ  1 / (k + rank_i(d))
#
# where:
#   k = 60 (empirical constant — dampens the advantage of rank 1 vs rank 2)
#   rank_i(d) = position of document d in list i (1-indexed; ∞ if absent)

# Example: "Physics article" is ranked #2 by FAISS and #1 by BM25:
rrf_physics = 1/(60+2) + 1/(60+1) = 0.01613 + 0.01639 = 0.03252

# "Quantum theory" is ranked #1 by BM25 but #3 by FAISS:
rrf_quantum = 1/(60+1) + 1/(60+3) = 0.01639 + 0.01587 = 0.03226

# Physics wins (0.03252 > 0.03226) because it's HIGH IN BOTH lists!
# Even though it was #1 in neither list individually.
  • The constant k=60 prevents rank #1 from dominating too much. With k=60, rank 1 gets score 1/61=0.0164, rank 2 gets 1/62=0.0161 — only a tiny difference. This means a passage at rank 5 in BOTH lists can beat a passage at rank 1 in only ONE list.Consensus is more important than being the top result in a single method.
  • RRF uses ONLY ranks, not raw scores. This solves the incompatibility problem: cosine scores ∈ [-1,1] and BM25 scores ∈ [0,30+] are on completely different scales. Ranks are always comparable.You can fuse ANY number of ranked lists regardless of their score scales.
polimibot/rag/fusion.py
def reciprocal_rank_fusion(ranked_lists, *, k=60, k_out=None):
    """Combine multiple ranked lists into one consensus ranking.
    
    Works for:
      - Dense + BM25 fusion (2 lists)
      - Multi-query fusion (5 lists — one per query variant)
      - Any number of retrievers
    """
    scores = {}  # chunk_id → accumulated RRF score
    for ranked_list in ranked_lists:
        for rank, (chunk, _original_score) in enumerate(ranked_list, start=1):
            key = (chunk.source, chunk.chunk_id)
            scores[key] = scores.get(key, 0.0) + 1.0 / (k + rank)
    
    # Sort by RRF score descending → best consensus passages first
    fused = sorted(scores.items(), key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return fused[:k_out]
Section 08

Score Gating — The Quality Bouncer

Think of a bouncer at a club. They check your ID (the retrieval score). If you pass the threshold → you're in (passages become context). If you don't → you're turned away, and the system tries a different approach (live search). The bouncer's job is to prevent bad context from entering the LLM's prompt — because irrelevant context is worse than no context at all.

Why is bad context worse than none? If you give an LLM a passage about "Rome, Georgia (USA)" when the question is about "Rome, Italy," the LLM might use that misleading context to pick the wrong answer. Without any context, it at least relies on its general knowledge (which might be correct). Bad retrieval actively hurts accuracy.

polimibot/strategies/rag_strategy.py — score gating logic
# After retrieval, check if the best passage is "good enough"
top_score = float(passages[0][1]) if passages else 0.0

# Different retrieval paths produce different score scales:
if self.use_reranker:
    active_threshold = self.min_score_rerank   # cross-encoder scale
elif self.use_hybrid:
    active_threshold = self.min_score_rrf      # RRF scale (~0.01-0.10)
else:
    active_threshold = self.min_score          # cosine scale (-1 to +1)

# The gate decision:
gated = (active_threshold is not None
         and (not passages or top_score < active_threshold))

# If gated = True → don't use these passages (they're unreliable)
# Instead → try live search or degrade to no-context baseline

Section 09

Live Search Fallback — Real-Time Wikipedia

Your textbook doesn't cover the topic? Google it on your phone. The offline index (your textbook) can't contain every possible Wikipedia article. When retrieval scores are too low (the bouncer rejected the passages), the system fires a real-time Wikipedia API query, fetches fresh articles, chunks them on-the-fly, and uses those as context instead.

polimibot/rag/live_search.py — simplified flow
class LiveSearchFallback:
    """Real-time Wikipedia search. Fires only when offline retrieval fails."""
    
    def search(self, query, *, category=None, timeout=5.0, max_articles=2):
        # 1. Query Wikipedia's search API for matching article titles
        titles = wikipedia.search(query, results=max_articles)
        # → ["Alexander Graham Bell", "Invention of the telephone"]
        
        # 2. Fetch each article's full text
        articles = []
        for title in titles:
            page = wikipedia.page(title)
            articles.append(LiveArticle(
                title=title,
                text=page.content,
                category=category,
            ))
        
        # 3. Return articles (will be chunked by RAGStrategy or IndexGrower)
        return articles
  • Hard 5-second timeout per query. The game server gives 30 seconds per question — we can't spend more than 5 on Wikipedia fetching.Time budget management: retrieval + live search + LLM scoring must all fit in 25 seconds.
  • Fetches at most 2 articles. More isn't better — it increases latency and may introduce noise.Precision over recall: better to have 2 highly relevant articles than 5 barely-relevant ones.
  • The question's category (history, science, etc.) is passed through so fetched articles can be tagged for future category filtering.When articles are later added to the permanent index, they carry their category label.

The learning loop: Live-fetched articles aren't just used once and thrown away. They're buffered in the IndexGrower. If the answer turns out correct, those articles are permanently added to the offline index. Next time a similar question appears → offline retrieval succeeds → no live search needed!

Section 10

Reranking — The Second Opinion

Think of a job hiring process. Step 1: HR quickly scans 500 resumes and selects 15 candidates (fast bi-encoder). Step 2: The hiring manager interviews those 15 in depth (slow cross-encoder). You'd never interview all 500 — too expensive. But you'd never hire from just the resume scan alone — too superficial. The two-stage approach gives you quality at speed.

Bi-Encoder (Fast, Approximate)

Encodes query and passage separately. The score is just the dot product between their independent vectors. Fast because passage vectors are pre-computed — only the query needs encoding at runtime.

Cross-Encoder (Slow, Precise)

Feeds query AND passage together into one model. The model sees both texts simultaneously and can attend across them — catching nuances the bi-encoder misses. But it requires a full forward pass per (query, passage) pair.

Bi-encoder only1 (query only) + dot products~50 ms ✅⭐⭐⭐

polimibot/rag/reranker.py — the reranking step
class CrossEncoderReranker:
    """Re-score retrieval candidates with joint query-passage attention."""
    
    def rerank(self, query: str, candidates, *, top_k=None):
        # Build (query, passage_text) pairs for joint encoding
        pairs = [(query, chunk.text) for chunk, _score in candidates]
        
        # Score all pairs (the expensive part — one forward pass per pair)
        scores = self._model.predict(pairs)  # BAAI/bge-reranker-base
        
        # DISCARD the original retrieval scores!
        # The cross-encoder score is strictly better.
        scored = [(chunk, float(s)) for (chunk, _), s in zip(candidates, scores)]
        scored.sort(key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
        
        return scored[:top_k] if top_k else scored
  • Each (query, passage) pair is concatenated and fed to the cross-encoder as a single input. The model has full attention across both texts.Unlike bi-encoder (which encodes independently), the cross-encoder can see: "Does this passage ACTUALLY answer this specific question?"
  • The original retrieval score (cosine or RRF) is thrown away! The cross-encoder provides a strictly better relevance judgment.Don't mix score scales. The cross-encoder has seen both texts together — its score supersedes the approximate bi-encoder score.
  • Returns only the top-k results after re-sorting. From 15 candidates → pick the best 3.The reranker narrows the pool: oversearch broadly (15), then precision-filter (3).
Section 11

LLM Answer Scoring — Making the Final Decision

You've found the right pages. Now read them and pick your answer. The LLM receives the formatted passages as "Reference material" and the question with options. It then either (a) directly reports which option it thinks is correct via probability inspection, or (b) writes out its reasoning step-by-step and concludes with a letter.

Mode A: Logit Scoring (Default — Fast + Precise)

How logit scoring works
# The prompt ends with "Answer:" and we peek at what the LLM
# would generate NEXT — without actually generating anything.

prompt = f"""Reference material:
{formatted_passages}

Question: In what year did Italy annex Rome?
A) 1861  B) 1870  C) 1882  D) 1895

Answer:"""

# One forward pass → read the probability distribution over next token:
P(next_token = "A") = 0.03
P(next_token = "B") = 0.89  ← highest!
P(next_token = "C") = 0.05
P(next_token = "D") = 0.03

# Result: chosen_index=1 (B), confidence=0.89
# Total cost: exactly ONE forward pass. No text generated.

Mode B: Free Generation (For Chain-of-Thought)

For CoT and ELIMINATION styles, the LLM generates reasoning text ("Let me think... passage [1] says 1870..."), then we regex-parse for the answer letter at the end. Slower (many forward passes) but allows the model to reason through complex questions.

Why logit scoring is preferred: (1) One forward pass instead of dozens. (2) Produces a clean probability distribution — no regex parsing needed. (3) The confidence values are calibrated and useful for ensemble fusion. (4) Never fails to produce an answer (no parse errors).

Section 12

The Self-Growing Index — Learning From Experience

Imagine a student who adds new pages to their textbook after each successful exam answer. "I found this Wikipedia article about the Rosetta Stone, used it to answer correctly, so I'll glue it into my textbook for next time." But if the answer was wrong? "Maybe that article was misleading — throw it away, don't add it." Over time, the textbook grows with verified, useful knowledge only.

The 4-Phase Lifecycle

polimibot/rag/index_grower.py — the learning cycle
class IndexGrower:
    """Self-growing knowledge index. Learns from correct answers."""
    
    def buffer(self, article: LiveArticle, question_id: str) -> list[Chunk]:
        """Phase 1: Store a live-fetched article in pending buffer.
        Chunks are available for the current question's context,
        but NOT yet in the permanent index."""
        chunks = chunk_text(article.text, source=article.title, ...)
        self._pending[question_id] = chunks
        return chunks
    
    def confirm(self, question_id: str) -> None:
        """Phase 2a: Answer was correct → promote to permanent index.
        Embeds the chunks and appends them to FAISS + BM25."""
        chunks = self._pending.pop(question_id, [])
        if not chunks:
            return
        # Embed with the SAME embedder used for the rest of the index
        embeddings = self._embedder.encode_passage([c.text for c in chunks])
        # Hot-append to the live in-memory indices
        self._retriever.append_chunks(chunks, embeddings)
        self._confirmed.extend(chunks)
    
    def discard(self, question_id: str) -> None:
        """Phase 2b: Answer was wrong → throw away the buffered chunks.
        Don't pollute the index with potentially misleading content."""
        self._pending.pop(question_id, None)  # silently drop
    
    def flush(self) -> None:
        """Phase 3: End of session → persist everything to disk.
        Re-saves the FAISS and BM25 files with the new chunks included."""
        if not self._confirmed:
            return  # nothing to flush
        self._index.save(self._index_path)
        self._bm25.save(self._bm25_path)
        # Append raw articles to corpus.jsonl for future rebuilds
  • Called by RAGStrategy when live search fires. The chunks are used for context RIGHT NOW (this question), but they're "on probation" — not yet permanent.Use the info immediately (we need it to answer) but wait for confirmation before committing.
  • Called by the Runner after the game server says "correct!" The chunks are embedded and appended to both FAISS and BM25 indices in memory. All FUTURE questions can now find these passages via normal retrieval.Only learn from success. Correct answers validate the supporting evidence.
  • Called by the Runner when the answer was wrong. The pending chunks are silently deleted. They never enter the index.Wrong answers might have been caused by misleading context. Don't poison the index with it.
  • Called once at game end. Persists all in-memory growth to disk files. Next game session starts with a bigger, better index.Memory is volatile — without flush, all learning is lost when the process exits.

The Runner orchestrates the learning loop: The RAGStrategy doesn't know if its answer was correct — that information comes from the game server AFTER submission. The Runner is the only component that sees both the strategy output AND the server's verdict. That's why confirm/discard are called from runner.py, not from the strategy itself.
